Abstract
The severe global water scarcity and record-high fossil oil price have greatly stimulated the research interests on new desalination technologies which can be driven by renewable energy or waste energy. In this study, a hybrid desalination process comprising freeze desalination and membrane distillation (FD-–MD) processes was developed and explored in an attempt to utilize the waste cold energy released from re-gasification of liquefied natural gas (LNG). The concept of this technology was demonstrated using indirect-contact freeze desalination (ICFD) and direct-contact membrane distillation (DCMD) configurations. By optimizing the ICFD operation parameters, namely, the usage of nucleate seeds, operation duration and feed concentration, high quality drinkable water with a low salinity ∼0.144 g/L was produced in the ICFD process. At the same time, using the optimized hollow fiber module length and packing density in the DCMD process, ultra pure water with a low salinity of 0.062 g/L was attained at a condition of high energy efficiency (EE). Overall, by combining FD and MD processes and adopting the optimized operation parameters, the hybrid FD-–MD system has been successfully demonstrated. A high total water recovery of 71.5% was achieved, and the water quality obtained met the standard for drinkable water. In addition, with results from specific energy calculation, it was proven that the hybrid process is an energy-saving process and utilization of LNG cold energy could greatly reduce the total energy consumption.

‘LNG cold energy’ is one type of waste energy released from the re-gasification of liquefied natural gas (LNG). The ‘cold energy’ refers to the heat absorption effect from the ambient surrounding when LNG is re-gasified at the LNG terminals (Miyazaki et al., 2000). For the reason of easy transportation and handling, natural gas (NG) is stored in a liquid form at atmospheric pressure and about 110 K (i.e., −162 °C) (Qiang et al., 2005). Hence, the liquid NG must be re-gasified before being supplied as a gaseous form in LNG terminals with the aid of heat drawing from the surroundings, which is generally supplied by sea water now. Owing to the relative low temperature, high energy density and blooming global LNG market, LNG cold energy is recognized as one high quality energy sources to cool media. This attracts many researchers 2.5 MB.2.5 MB to carry out works on the energy density and design of efficient heat exchange equipment. Unfortunately, most cold energy during LNG re-gasification has not been utilized until today, owing to the industrial preference on the ‘hot energy’ source (Remeljej and Hoadley, 2006). The term ‘hot energy’ refers to high temperature energy sources which can release heat to the industrial processes.
In this work, we propose and explore a new concept of a MD based hybrid desalination technology: freeze desalination-membrane distillation (FD-–MD) using LNG waste cold energy. MD is a thermal-driven process based on the principle of vapourvapor–-liquid equilibrium and heat and mass transfer. During the MD process, the volatile compounds in the hot feed solution evaporate at the feed side, diffuse through the hydrophobic membrane 2.5 MB. pores and condense into the liquid flow at the distillate side (Teoh and Chung, 2009). The non-volatile salts are isolated and left in the feed solution.

Considering the characteristics and mechanisms of FD and MD processes, both systems require large quantities of high quality cold energy. For the FD process, cold energy is needed to cool and crystallize the pre-cooled saline feed solution; while in the MD process, cold energy is required to continuously cool and maintain the low temperature of the distillate stream. Therefore, the large amount of cold energy from re-gasification of LNG can be effectively used in the design of FD-–MD processes to minimize the overall energy consumption.
Besides utilization of waste LNG cold energy, several additional benefits are foreseen by integrating these two processes. First, the overall recovery of seawater feed will be much higher as compared with a single FD process. Limited by the eutectic temperature of the salt/water mixture (−21.1 °C), the FD process has a theoretical maximum of water recovery (∼16%) for 3.5 wt% seawater (Qin et al., 2009). By treating the discharged brine from FD with MD, the TWR is expected to be significantly higher than other integrated systems and commercially available technologies (e.g. RO and MED). Secondly, energy consumption can be further reduced by appropriate design and integration of cold and hot streams in the hybrid process. As one example shown in Fig. 1, the purified water stream from FD (around 0 °C) can be used to cool the inlet of the MD distillate (Shi and Che, 2009).
Therefore, in this work, we aim to study the feasibility of the FD-–MD hybrid desalination process comprising indirect contact freeze desalination (ICFD) and direct contact membrane distillation (DCMD) configurations. Prior to the hybrid process experiments, the effects of their key operation parameters on water recovery, produced water salinity, and overall energy efficiency will also be investigated. Thereafter, the variation of brine concentration, product water quality and TWR will be evaluated in order to optimize the integrated system. Eventually, the feasibility of LNG cold energy utilization in the hybrid FD-–MD process will be tested.

The FD separation process is closely related to the phase transition behaviour behavior of a salt-water mixture. In order to understand the FD process, a sodium chloride (NaCl) - –water binary solution is used to illustrate the working mechanism. As shown by the arrows in Fig. 3, for a binary solution containing 3.5 wt% NaCl, water starts to transit from the liquid phase to a solid crystal form when the temperature is decreased and lower than the freezing point. In general, the FD process consists of four steps: (a) pre-cool the feed brine, (b) crystallize the ice crystals, (c) separate ice crystals from the brine solution and (d) wash and melt ice crystals to obtain high purity water (Rice and Chau, 1997). In literature, many studies on the FD process have been focused on the direct contact freeze desalination (DCFD) process due to its operation efficiency and cost-saving. The so-called ‘direct contact’ refers to the intimate mixing between the feed and the refrigerant (e.g., iso-butane) (Wiegandt et al., 1987). However, this application is constrained by the safety concerns during the handling of the explosive refrigerant. In recent years, safer FD configurations such as indirect contact freeze desalination (ICFD) and vacuum freeze desalination (FD) have been developed. In this study, an ICFD process is employed by installing a heat exchange device externally to the crystallizer unit to cool and maintain the temperature. The details of the ICFD set-up are described in Section 3.3.
2.2 MD and DCMD
MD is a thermal-driven transport process where a membrane is used as a barrier to separate distillate water from the feed solution. Normally, the feed side is operated at a relatively high temperature (40–80 °C) while the distillate side is maintained at a low temperature (15–30 °C). During the experiment, as shown in Fig. 2, water from the hot feed vaporizes at the membrane feed side, transports through membrane pores and finally condenses at the permeate side.

The entire hybrid FD-–MD process flows are shown in Fig. 1. Seawater was firstly treated by the ICFD process. Purified water was obtained from the melted ice. 4 Results and discussion
4.1 ICFD experiments
In order to operate the hybrid FD-–MD system efficiently, FD and MD operational conditions were investigated separately. In this section, the variables incurred in the FD operation include nucleate seeds, operation duration and feed brine salinity. We optimized these factors to produce ice crystals with a higher purity and yield.
4.1.1 Effect of nucleate seeds addition
Two modes of FD operation, ‘normal mode’ and ‘nucleation mode’, have been examined in the ICFD experiments. Fig. 6a) shows the evolution of temperature in the crystallizer cylinder under the ‘normal mode’ using a 35 g/L NaCl solution as the feed. The solution temperature decreases and finally reaches −6.7 °C because the super-saturated solution has reached the metastable limit (Mullin, 2001). Starting from this point, it is observed that a large quantity of ice crystals form within seconds and the solution temperature simultaneously increases from −6.7 °C to −2.1 °C.
Fig. 6 The temperature evolution of EG bath and brine feed solution. (a): set temperature: −10 °C, 3.5 %wt% NaCl, normal mode. (b): setting temperature: −10 °C, 3.5 %wt NaCl, nucleation mode.
As the nucleation rate and the growth rate of crystals in a super-cooled solution follow an exponential function of the degree of super-cooling (Garside et al., 2002), it is believed that the sudden formation of ice crystals is driven by the big temperature difference between the low temperature (−6.7 °C) and the equilibrium temperature (−2.1 °C). The increase in solution temperature from −6.7 °C to −2.1 °C can be simply attributed to the fusion heat released from state transition. This finding is in good agreement with a recent study by Qin et al., where a steep increase of solution temperature was found when spontaneous nucleation took place in a super-cooled solution (Qin et al., 2009).
Surprisingly, by incorporating 20 g nucleate seeds, as depicted in Fig. 6b), the temperature profile of the brine varies notably. When the nucleate seeds are added at the freezing point (−2.1 °C), the ice crystals form continuously and the quantity of crystals increases gradually with the operation duration. It is also seen that the solution temperature decreases slowly together with the crystal growth, which indicates a low-degree of super-saturation.
The different ice formation behaviours behaviors observed with/without nucleate seeding could be understood from the aspect of crystallization kinetics as demonstrated in Fig. 7 (Tiller, 1991). In an ice-water mixture, the critical size for the crystals to form is defined as the minimum embryo size which allows the embryo to grow spontaneously. The value of crystal size depends on the free energy barrier generated from the combined counter effect of volume free energy (GV) and surface free energy (GS). When the feed solution reaches its freezing point, a trace amount of small ice crystalline lattices is generated. However, as the lattice size of these crystals is smaller than the critical size, the total Gibbs free energy of the system (GT) tends to increase with lattice size preferentially. As a result, these crystalline lattices continuously re-dissolve into the solution. Therefore, if a small amount of ice crystals larger than the critical size are added into the solution at its super-saturation state, the added ice crystals will be shed as the secondary nuclei, while the embryo ice nucleate crystals grow spontaneously as driven by the negative GT.

With the same operation duration of 0.5 h, the quality of the ice generated from ‘nucleation mode’ is much better than that obtained from ‘normal mode’. With nucleation, the purity of the ice is tremendously improved to 0.104 g/L. The improvement in ice quality can be credited to the distinguished temperature profiles and crystal formation and growth processes. In the ‘normal mode’, significant variations in super-saturation occur and in consequence, the produced ice contains large interlocking crystals, agglomerates and fines (Mullin, 2001). As a result, a large amount of salts are trapped within the interlocked crystal structures. Whilst in ‘nucleation mode’, the formation and growth of ice crystals are induced by the added nucleate seeds and the low degree of super-saturation does not cause significant interlocking between crystals. Therefore, the majority of the salts are rejected when the regular-packed ice crystals are formed and the salt residue on crystal surface can be readily washed off.
4.1.2 Effect of FD operation duration
Under the nucleation mode, the effect of FD operation duration was also examined and the performance is listed in Table 3a). As shown, the ice salinity increases with a longer operation. When FD is operated longer, the brine solution is concentrated and thus, the free energy gap between the ice phase and the solution phase decreases (Qin et al., 2009). Hence, more salt residue is trapped in the ice crystals, which results in an increase in salinity. It is also found that there is a critical operation duration at which the ice salinity increases sharply. In the first 2 h, the ice crystals appear as clusters suspending in the crystallizer unit. With a longer duration (∼2.5 h), besides the suspended clusters, an unexpected thick ice layer is also observed on the inner wall of the unit. Though the ice layer is also processed by the washing step, a significant amount of salts is still retained and eventually results in a high salinity.
The morphologies of ice crystals obtained from durations of 0.5 h, 1 h and 2 h are depicted in Fig. 8. Generally, all ice clusters are of a thin plate shape. The sizes of ice particles are in the range of 0.5–1.5 mm in dimension and there is no visible difference for the size of the ice obtained with different operation durations. This phenomenon might be explained by the kinetic theory of crystallization process. Due to the stirrer agitation and crystal collision, there is a kinetic equilibrium between crystal nucleation growth and break up. As a result, the crystal size is limited (Zauner and Jones, 2000; Garside, 1977) (Fig. 9).
4.1.3 Effect of feed salinity
In the nucleation mode, the effect of feed salinity on FD performance was also investigated and the results are listed in Table 3b). Four feed concentrations including 15 g/L, 35 g/L, 45 g/L and 60 g/L were selected and compared. In general, the FD process is effective for the purification of brines of different salinities and the resultant clean water salinity increases with the feed salinity. Except for the 60 g/L feed, the salinities of obtained water are all within the requirement of drinkable water (0.500 g/L).
4.2 DCMD experiments
This section focuses on the optimization of the MD operation parameters. The FD discharged brine with a high salt concentration is utilized as the feed to the MD process.
4.2.1 Membrane properties
High performance single-layer hollow fiber membranes were fabricated as MD modules. Fig. 10 displays the cross-section morphology of the prepared module. It can be observed that the fiber has a wall thickness around 190 μm. Micro pores with inter-connected pore structure are obtained, which is owing to the addition of the pore-forming additive (EG) (Tomaszewska, 1996). In addition, by employing weak internal and external coagulants, few macrovoids are formed across the membrane cross-section (Teoh and Chung, 2009). These micro-structure characteristics play an important role in enhancing MD flux while maintaining membranes' mechanical strength and operational stability (García-Payo et al., 2010).
Other membrane properties are summarized in Table 2b). The membrane has an average pore size of 0.245 μm, which indicates that the diffusion of water vapour vapor through the membrane pores combines the mechanisms of molecular diffusion and Knudsen diffusion (Schofield et al., 1987). Credited to the incorporation of super-hydrophobic PTFE particles, the membranes exhibit a high contact angle of 108°, which could improve the membranes' wetting resistance and long-term operation durability.
4.2.2 Effect of module length
Fig. 10 shows the variations of permeation flux and energy efficiency (EE) with feed inlet temperature and module length, respectively. Three module lengths are tested; they are 15 cm, 25 and 35 cm. All modules have the same packing density of ∼20%.
As shown in Fig. 10a), the permeation fluxes increase with the feed temperature slightly nonlinearly for these three modules. The nonlinearity relationship observed is attributed to the exponential increase of water vapour vapor pressure upon the increase of temperature. At a certain inlet temperature, the increase of module length appears to reduce the permeation flux. This decreasing phenomenon becomes more pronounced at higher feed temperatures. As shown in Fig. 10b), at 50 °C, with an increase in module length from 15 cm to 35 cm, the flux decreases by 1.5 kg m−2 h−1. However, it reduces by 7.9 kg m−2 h−1 when the feed temperature is 80 °C. The phenomena is owing to the fact that the higher feed temperature could result in the higher conductive heat flux and therefore, accelerate the loss of driving force along the long module.
EE is defined as the percentage of the effective heat flux (vaporization heat Ql) to the total heat flux (Edwie et al., 2012). A higher EE value is an indication of lower energy consumption for one unit of clean water produced. Hence, it is highly desired to design the membrane module with a higher EE value.
Fig. 10c) demonstrates the variation of the EE value versus feed temperature. Generally, EE increases with feed temperature. As the total heat flux is the sum of vaporization heat flux (Ql) and conductive heat flux (QC), the variation of EE depends on the relative changing rates of these two heat fluxes. As the feed temperature increases, the nonlinear enhancement of permeation flux introduces the exponential increment of Ql, whereas QC follows the linear augmentation (Wang P. et al., 2011). As a result, an increase in feed temperature eventually results in an increase in EE value. Fig. 10d) shows the plot of EE value versus module length. EE increases slightly with increasing module length. This is because when the module length increases, the outlet temperature of the distillate side increases significantly due to the heat dissipation across the membranes. A higher distillate outlet temperature reduces the temperature gradient between the feed and the distillate sides. Therefore, QC decreases and the resultant EE increases.

Fig. 11a) and c) show the variations of permeation flux and EE with the feed inlet temperatures. Fig. 11b) and d) demonstrate the changes of permeation flux and EE with packing density, it can be seen that a higher packing density could result in a lower permeation flux but a higher EE value, especially when a high feed temperature is employed.

4.3 FD-–MD hybrid experiments
With the optimized conditions from FD and MD experiments, the desalination performance of the hybrid process was examined. The FD process with nucleation and 2 h operation duration is chosen for the hybrid experiment due to its high yield and acceptable water purity. The hollow fiber module with a module length of 35 cm and packing density of 30% is selected as the DCMD module owing to its high thermal efficiency and production rate. The inlet temperatures of the feed and permeate solutions are maintained at 60.2 °C and 15.5 °C, respectively. The flowrates of feed and permeate solutions are kept at 2.0 L/min and 0.3 L/min, respectively.
Fig. 13 shows the variation of permeation flux and salt concentration of the brine with time. It can be seen that with a longer operation duration, the concentration of NaCl in the brine increases at an accelerated speed. Simultaneously, the permeation flux decreases by ∼20%. It can be observed that the loss of flux is much smaller as compared with that in an RO concentration experiments (García-Payo et al., 2010). This is because the less sensitivity of vapour vapor pressure in the MD process as compared with the osmotic pressure in the RO process. Unlike at lower salt concentrations, where only temperature polarization (TP) plays a determining role in DCMD flux, the loss of permeation flux at higher salt concentrations is caused by both TP and CP effects at the feed brine (Drioli et al., 2006a,b; Mariah et al., 2006; Al Obaidani et al., 2008). For the TP effect at higher salt concentrations, scaling and deposition of salt on the membrane surface could increase the membrane thermal conductivity (Wang P. et al., 2011). Due to this effect, the effective temperature difference at the membrane surfaces is smaller as compared with low salt concentrations. The CP effect refers to the decrease in effective driving force caused by the higher salt concentration at membrane surface. The concentration gap between the surface and bulk solution can be estimated using equation (7) with a known flux, bulk salt concentration and boundary layer information. In our case, the concentrated brine could cause more severe CP. This would result in a lower water activity and consequently, compromise the vapour vapor pressure at membrane surface.
Fig. 14 summarizes the overall flow of the hybrid FD-–MD process. With 8 kg feed of synthetic seawater (3.5 wt% NaCl solution), 1.46 kg (18.25%) fresh water with 0.146 g/L salinity is produced from the FD unit, while another 4.26 kg (53.25%) with 0.062 g/L salinity is produced from the DCMD unit. With the contribution from both units, the hybrid process exhibits a high TWR of 71.5%, which is much higher than the value of the typical SWRO operated under 50 bar pressure (37.5%–50.0%) (Escobar, 2010).
During the entire experiment of hybrid process, the temperatures of Freon® at the outlet of condenser and inlet of compressor are very stable. The cold energy provided is enough to support and maintain the coolers. From the performance of this hybrid experiment, it is confirmed that the cold energy released from Freon® R23 gasification can be effectively used to cool the coolant streams from the FD crystallizer and MD distillate cooler. Considering the relative close re-gasification behaviour behavior of Freon® R23 and NG (Perry, 1984) the application of LNG cold energy in the FD-–MD hybrid process is very promising and with high feasibility. However, in order to further evaluate the application, more experiments and calculation need to be carried based on real LNG vaporizer.

In order to obtain and understand the energy consumption of the hybrid FD-–MD process, an evaluation of specific energy consumption (SEC) for fresh water production is made based on the operational conditions in Section 4.3. Considering that the hybrid FD-–MD process involves several hot and cold streams; the accurate SEC value can only be obtained after a careful optimization of the heat exchange network (HEN) design. As the calculation and HEN design are not key findings of this work, the details are included in the supplementary materials.
Based on the HEN design, three heat exchangers and extra heaters and coolers need to be installed in the hybrid system to reach the minimum energy consumption. The SEC value based on the simulation results is 2.343 kwh/m3 if only heating power is counted. The value increases to 4.633 kwh/m3 when both heating and cooling power is counted. Compared with the calculated SEC value of RO units (about 3.5 kwh/m3 for 3.5 wt% feed salinity and 50% TWR), the value is very attractive if the cooling energy is provided by LNG cold energy (Avlonitis et al., 2003; Escobar, 2010; Lattemann et al., 2010). Furthermore, it proves that the utilization of LNG cold energy could dramatically reduce the total energy consumption of the hybrid process. Compared with other proposed hybrid processes summarized in Table 1, the FD-–MD process shows a similar or even higher TWR value, while the SEC value appears lower. However, since several different estimation methods are applied, more accurate comparison can only be made based on similar estimation or calculation methodology.
5 Conclusions
In this work, a hybrid freeze desalination-membrane distillation (FD-–MD) process which utilizes waste cold energy for desalination is designed and systematically explored. The following conclusions can be drawn:
1.
The concept of hybrid FD-–MD process has been demonstrated by integrating indirect-contact freeze desalination (ICFD) and direct contact membrane distillation (DCMD) units. The DCMD process is employed to treat the brine discharged from the FD unit. Drinkable clean water has been successfully produced by the hybrid process with a total water recovery of 71.5%.
2.
In ICFD experiments, the addition of ice nucleate is found to be a useful method to alter the crystallization kinetics in the ICFD experiments. It is also found that the ice salinity tends to be higher with a longer operation time. In addition, it has been demonstrated that the FD unit is capable of desalting saline water containing different salt concentrations.
3.
In DCMD experiments, ultra-pure water is produced using the high performance hollow fiber membranes. It is found that the permeation flux is lower while the energy efficiency (EE) is higher when a longer membrane module or a higher packing density is applied.
4.
Hybrid FD-–MD experiments have been performed at the selected FD and MD operation parameters using FD discharged brine as the feed for MD. There is an accelerated increase of salt concentration in the retentate of MD, thus permeation flux of water shows a continuous decrease.
5.
The results from specific energy consumption have verified the energy-saving characteristic of the hybrid system. Less energy is required as compared with the current technology if cold energy is available.
6.
In order to fully harvest the benefits of the FD-–MD hybrid process, further studies will be focused on three aspects in our subsequent experiments, namely, 1) test running of pilot-scale FD-–MD combined with a LNG vaporizer, 2) optimization and economic evaluation of the hybrid system and 3) investigation of multiple salts effects on the performance of FD-–MD systems.
